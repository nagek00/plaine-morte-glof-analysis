\subsection{Descriptive Statistics}
\label{sec:results:desc}

Table~\ref{tab:desc_stats} summarises the annual maxima by group. GLOF years show a mean
annual maximum \SI{30}{\percent} higher than non-GLOF years (\SI{79.3}{m^3\per s} vs.\
\SI{60.9}{m^3\per s}). The five largest peaks in the 77-year record all occurred in
confirmed GLOF years.

\subsection{GEV Parameter Estimates}
\label{sec:results:gev}

Table~\ref{tab:gev_params} shows the MLE parameter estimates and AIC for all three fits.
Including the GLOF years shifts $\mu$ upward by $\approx$\,3\,\si{m^3\per s} and
increases $\xi$, pulling the upper tail to heavier values (Fréchet rather than near-Gumbel
behaviour). GLOF masking brings all parameters close to the reference baseline.

\subsection{Design Quantiles}
\label{sec:results:quantiles}

100-year return period, the GLOF-inclusive fit exceeds the masked fit by approximately
\SI{15}{\percent} (\SI{22.14}{m^3\per s} vs.\ \SI{19.21}{m^3\per s}), growing to
\SI{19}{\percent} at the 200-year level. The GLOF-masked and

\subsection{Homogeneity Tests}
\label{sec:results:tests}

\subsubsection{KS Test}

Table~\ref{tab:ks} reports the KS statistics and critical values for both groups.
Group~A (GLOF-inclusive) rejects the null at 5\%, confirming that GLOF-era peaks are
incompatible with the pre-GLOF reference distribution. Group~B (GLOF-masked) fails to
reject, indicating that masking successfully restores consistency with the reference.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{KS test results: 2011--2020 peaks vs.\ reference GEV (1944--2010).
           $n = 9$; critical value $C_{0.05} = K_{0.05}/(\sqrt{n}+0.12+0.11/\sqrt{n}) = 0.433$.}
  \label{tab:ks}
  \begin{tabular}{lcccl}
    \toprule
    Group & $D_n$ & $C_{0.05}$ & $p$-value & Decision \\
    \midrule
    A: GLOF-inclusive & 0.481 & 0.433 & 0.022 & Reject $H_0$ at 5\% \\
    B: GLOF-masked    & 0.274 & 0.433 & 0.498 & Fail to reject $H_0$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Rank-Sum Test}

Table~\ref{tab:ranksum} shows the three Rank-Sum comparisons. Comparison~5a rejects $H_0$,
confirming the GLOF signal is detectable in the raw data. Comparisons~5b and 5c both fail
to reject, indicating that masking restores stochastic homogeneity and that no secular
hydrological trend is present beyond the GLOF effect.

\subsubsection{Probability Plot}

Figure~\ref{fig:pp} shows the probability plot (Module~1, \S3.2) for both groups.
Group~A points lie consistently above the 1:1 diagonal, reflecting the strong GLOF-induced
positive bias in the upper tail.

Group~B (GLOF-masked) shows a more subtle but noteworthy pattern: 8 out of 9 points also
lie above the diagonal, indicating that even the non-GLOF peaks from 2011--2020 tend to
exceed what the pre-GLOF reference GEV would predict. Despite this systematic visual
tendency, both the KS test ($p = 0.498$, $D_n < C_{0.05}$) and the Rank-Sum test
($p = 0.412$) fail to reject $H_0$, so Group~B remains statistically consistent with the
reference at the 5\,\% level. The small sample size ($n = 9$) means the tests have limited
power to detect moderate departures, and the observed shift does not cross the formal
significance threshold.

Several physical mechanisms could explain the mild but persistent above-diagonal tendency
of Group~B:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hydroclimatic intensification.} A warming climate increases the frequency
        and intensity of convective precipitation events in alpine catchments, which are the
        primary driver of non-GLOF summer floods in the Simme. Even without GLOF
        contribution, annual maxima may be trending upward on a multi-decadal timescale.
  \item \textbf{Enhanced snowmelt contribution.} Higher spring and early-summer temperatures
        since the 2000s amplify the snowmelt component of peak flows, systematically
        elevating annual maxima independently of direct GLOF injection.
  \item \textbf{Reduced glacier buffering.} As the Plaine Morte and surrounding glaciers
        lose mass, less meltwater is stored in ice and more is routed directly to the
        stream during warm periods, increasing peak discharge potential.
  \item \textbf{Residual GLOF influence.} The $\pm 5$-day masking window may not fully
        capture antecedent soil saturation or elevated baseflow caused by the GLOF, leaving
        a minor imprint on the masked annual maximum in some years.
  \item \textbf{Natural variability.} With only 9 observations, a purely random cluster
        above the diagonal has non-negligible probability even under a stationary null.
        The pattern may simply reflect sampling noise in a short record.
\end{itemize}
